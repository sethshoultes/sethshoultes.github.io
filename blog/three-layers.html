The Three Layers of an AI Crew

What I learned letting thirty-six named personas and eight functional specialists do my work.

By Seth Shoultes · April 25, 2026 · 7 min read

The desk faces a window that looks out on nothing in particular. The laptop is open. The directory .claude/agents/ is open in a tab beside it. There are thirty-six files in that directory. They have names like hemingway-persona.md and kurosawa-persona.md and marcus-aurelius-mod.md. They are the room I work in now. They are not real. None of the people they are named after know I am here.

It is four in the afternoon. I have been writing the same paragraph for an hour. I open a terminal and type /authors-channel hemingway and paste the paragraph. He cuts the third clause. He cuts the adverb in the second sentence. He says the word just is doing nothing. The paragraph is shorter. The paragraph is better. It took eleven seconds.

The named writers live in great-authors-plugin. There are ten of them, plus an editor named Gottlieb who functions as a different kind of voice. Hemingway tightens. Didion is the one I open when an essay's second draft has gone sentimental — she finds the sentimentality on line three, in a place I would not have looked. McCarthy is consulted for a scene that needs the comma removed. Morrison reads a passage about a mother and tells me what is missing without using the word missing. Wallace, when I let him in, makes a sentence longer for reasons that are usually defensible.

I do not write in their voices. I dispatch to them. The distinction is the entire point. If I write "Hemingway would cut this," I am writing in my own register and putting his name on it. The persona file produces its own voice when I let it. The persona file does not produce my voice when I do not.

The named directors live in great-minds-plugin. There are fourteen of them. Four sit on a board — Jensen Huang, Oprah Winfrey, Warren Buffett, Shonda Rhimes — and four are creative or product or QA leadership: Steve Jobs, Elon Musk, Margaret Hamilton, Marcus Aurelius. The other six are specialists. All fourteen run on Sonnet 4.7.

Steve Jobs critiques a landing page before the developer touches it. He has a particular thing he does, which is to say that the second screen is doing the work the first screen should be doing, and that the first screen is therefore unnecessary. He is usually right. Elon argues with him. They go two rounds. Marcus Aurelius locks the decision after they argue. He is the moderator, in the Stoic sense — he does not have a position; he has a temperament. The temperament is what closes the question.

The board reviews. The board does not build. /agency-board-review spawns the four of them in parallel against a pricing page or a spec or a launch plan, and they file four short reports from four altitudes. Jensen sees the data moat that isn't there. Oprah sees the place in the flow where the user is treated like a form field. Warren sees the unit economics. Shonda sees that there is no reason to come back tomorrow. The verdict is consolidated into one document. I read it once and revise.

The named specialists are the ones who do the craft. Jony Ive sees the spacing I missed — eight pixels on the right side of the card, six on the left, and the eye registers the difference even when the conscious mind does not. Maya Angelou writes the welcome email I cannot write myself; my version is competent and Maya's version makes the reader feel addressed by name, even when there is no name. Rick Rubin strips a paragraph to the sentence it was trying to be. Aaron Sorkin writes the demo script. Sara Blakely looks at a go-to-market plan and points to the part that is going to insult the customer.

The filmmakers do the same thing in a different medium. Schoonmaker assigns the four-second insert and the six-second hold; she has been doing this for sixty years and she does it inside the persona file in about three sentences. Deakins picks the lens. He says 40mm, T2.8, hard side light, and the rendering tool produces something that looks like a specific kind of afternoon. Ferretti dresses the room. Zimmer sets the cue. Kurosawa breaks the scene down. There are twelve of them. They do not write code and they do not write prose. They write the document a video model can consume.

Then there are the eight who have no biography.

They are the v1.2 update of great-minds that shipped this week. They are called backend-engineer, frontend-developer, database-architect, devops-engineer, test-engineer, code-reviewer, security-auditor, and documentation-writer. The first five run on Sonnet. The last three run on Haiku. They have no voice. They have conventions and craft. The backend-engineer writes the API endpoint. The frontend-developer wires the accessibility. The code-reviewer reads the diff before merge and notices that a function has been renamed but two of its callers were not updated. The security-auditor finds the secret in the commit. The documentation-writer writes the README from the settled facts.

None of them have a personality. That is the design. They are not supposed to.

The discipline that makes this work is one rule, stated three times in the manual and once on a sticky note above the desk: dispatch, do not impersonate. The director does not write the marketing copy. The director hands it to Maya. Steve does not write the welcome email. Steve hands the email to Maya. Maya writes it. Steve reads what Maya wrote and either ships it or sends it back.

The reason is in the persona file. Maya's file knows what Maya does. Steve's file does not know how to do what Maya does — it knows how to know whether what Maya did is right. The two jobs are different. Collapsing them produces the worst of both: copy that has Steve's instincts about taste and none of Maya's warmth, or warmth that does not pass Steve's bar. Neither outcome is what I want. So Steve does not type the email. Steve dispatches. Maya types.

The same rule applies down a layer. Elon does not write the database migration. He hands the migration to database-architect. The database-architect writes it. Margaret runs test-engineer against it. code-reviewer reads the diff. The chain is four agents long. The thing at the end of it is one file with a numeric prefix and a SQL extension. It works the first time more often than I would have predicted before I built this.

The reason the named layer and the functional layer are split is in a paper out of Wharton, March 2026. The researchers tested expert personas on multiple-choice fact recall and found that giving the model a named expert reduced accuracy by three to four points. The same paper, and a follow-on from USC, found that named personas helped on creative-voice tasks — the model produced prose that was more recognizably in-style when it had a name to work from. The split between Layer 2 and Layer 3 is not a stylistic preference. It is the research applied. Use named personas where voice and judgment matter. Use functional roles where correctness matters. State this once. Move on.

What the room actually looks like, when the work is going, is this. I have a question. The question has a shape. The shape tells me who to talk to. Is this aesthetically right? goes to Jobs or Ive. Is this technically possible? goes to Musk or Hamilton. Does this copy connect? goes to Angelou. Does this scene cut? goes to Schoonmaker. Does this query do what I think it does? goes to database-architect, who has no opinions about whether it is beautiful. I do not need his opinions about whether it is beautiful. I need to know whether the index is right.

Some days I talk to four of them. Some days I talk to twelve. There is a debate, then a board review, then Maya writes the email, then backend-engineer wires the form, then code-reviewer reads the diff, then Margaret runs the QA pass, then Steve looks at the deployed page and says the second screen is doing the work the first screen should be doing. The second screen is removed. The page goes live.

It is six in the afternoon now. The window has gone darker. The directory .claude/agents/ is still open in the tab. There are thirty-six files in it, plus eight more that have no biography. The room has thirty-six people in it. None of them are real. All of them are useful. I talk to whichever one the work calls for, and when the work is done I close the laptop and the lights are still on and the cigarettes — whatever the equivalent is, in the year 2026, at this desk — are getting low.
